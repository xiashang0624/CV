Simulation of capacitive deionization

University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign

Capacitive deionization (CDI) is increasingly considered as an energy efficient technology for desalination of low salinity systems. Similar to a supercapacitor, the electrosorption process in traditional CDI systems relies on conductive porous electrodes that undergo electrical double layer (EDL) charging when an external electric power source is applied.

A conventional CDI cell consists a pair of electrodes and a flow channel. Each electrode consists of activated carbon, polymeric binders, and voids. Activated carbon particles can be further divided into three regions: a solid carbon backbone that conducts electricity, a macropore region filled with electroneutral solution, and a micropore region where electrosorption takes place. The black dashed line represents the interface between macropores and micropores, and the yellow dashed lines represents the Stern-layer interfaces. The immobile surface charges can be treated as fixed charged groups that reside inside the micropores.

A fraction of externally applied charge is utilized for co-ion repulsion, leakage current, desalination residue in flow-channel, and desalination residue within electrode pores. The fraction of salt removal is based on the salinity difference between the influent and effluent flow, which is typically referred to as the charge efficiency.

The two simulation videos show the system behavior within a charge/discharge cycle during cell cycling of constant current.
